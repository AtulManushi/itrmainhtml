A Nidhi Company is a type of Non-Banking Financial Company (NBFC) recognized under Section 406 of the Companies Act, 2013. Its primary objective is to cultivate the habit of thrift and savings among its members by accepting deposits and providing loans exclusively to them. Operating on the principle of mutual benefit, Nidhi Companies are regulated by the Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) and are exempt from the Reserve Bank of India's core NBFC regulations, provided they adhere to the Nidhi Rules, 2014.

To establish a Nidhi Company, a minimum of seven members and three directors are required, along with a minimum paid-up equity share capital of ‚Çπ10 lakh. The company must be incorporated as a public limited company and include "Nidhi Limited" in its name. Within one year of incorporation, it must have at least 200 members. Nidhi Companies are restricted from issuing preference shares, opening branches outside their state of registration without prior approval, and engaging in activities like chit funds or leasing finance. They are permitted to accept deposits and provide loans only to their members, typically against securities such as gold, property, or fixed deposits.

A Nidhi Company is essentially a mutual benefit society, and its primary source of income is the interest earned on loans extended to its members. The interest rates on loans and the returns on deposits are regulated to ensure fairness and sustainability within the member community. Since they deal only with their members and use their funds, the risk of financial mismanagement is relatively lower compared to other NBFCs. Their operations are transparent and governed by the Nidhi Rules, 2014, which outline eligibility criteria, capital requirements, and limits on deposits and loans.

The primary advantage of a Nidhi Company is that it provides easy access to loans at lower interest rates compared to traditional banks, especially for small borrowers and those in rural or semi-urban areas. It encourages its members to develop a habit of saving regularly and offers a safe environment for their investments. The internal management is simpler, and there is minimal intervention from regulatory authorities like the RBI, provided the company complies with the established rules and guidelines.

However, Nidhi Companies also have limitations. They are not allowed to engage in activities such as leasing finance, hire purchase, or insurance. Their scope of operation is confined to their members, and they cannot solicit deposits or offer loans to the general public. Furthermore, they must maintain a net owned fund to deposit ratio of 1:20 and are required to invest at least 10% of their deposit liabilities in unencumbered term deposits with scheduled commercial banks or post offices to ensure liquidity.

Despite these restrictions, Nidhi Companies play a vital role in India's financial inclusion landscape. They offer a formalized avenue for thrift and credit to populations often underserved by mainstream financial institutions. With proper governance, ethical practices, and member participation, these companies can grow sustainably while meeting the financial needs of their communities. Their success largely depends on trust, community involvement, and adherence to legal and financial norms.
