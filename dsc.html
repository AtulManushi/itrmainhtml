A digital signature is a mathematical and cryptographic technique used to validate the authenticity, integrity, and origin of a digital message, document, or software. It functions much like a handwritten signature or a stamped seal, but it offers far more security and reliability, especially in digital communication. Digital signatures are widely used across industries to ensure that data exchanged over the internet remains secure and trustworthy.

The foundation of digital signatures lies in asymmetric encryption, which involves two separate but mathematically linked keys: a private key and a public key. The private key is kept secret by the signer, while the public key is shared with others to verify the authenticity of the signature. When a person or system signs a message or document digitally, a cryptographic hash (a unique fixed-length value representing the content) is created from the original data. This hash is then encrypted using the signer‚Äôs private key, forming the digital signature.

When the recipient receives the signed document, they use the sender's public key to decrypt the digital signature and obtain the original hash. Simultaneously, the recipient also generates a new hash from the received message. If the decrypted hash and the newly generated hash match, it confirms that the message has not been altered in transit and that it truly came from the claimed sender. If they do not match, the message is either tampered with or the signature is invalid.

One of the most important aspects of digital signatures is data integrity. Even a tiny change in the document, such as an extra space or altered character, will result in a completely different hash value. This makes it impossible for someone to modify the data without detection. This property makes digital signatures essential for secure communication and data exchange in fields like finance, law, healthcare, and government.
